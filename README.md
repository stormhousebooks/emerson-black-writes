## AUTHOR IDENTITY

**Author Name:** Emerson Black 
**Genre:** YA / Crossover NA Mystery-Thriller 
**Target Audience:** 15–25 years old (primary 16–22 crossover readership) 
**Publishing Model:** Fully self-published 
**Long-Term Goal:** 6-book core series + spin-offs. Build toward TV adaptation and possible traditional publishing deal or screen rights sale.

Emerson Black writes warm, witty, high-stakes mystery-thrillers centred on friendship, loyalty, scandal, and power structures. Stories blend amateur sleuthing with elite social systems (sport, media, academia, celebrity culture). The humour is character-driven and earns the drama — readers laugh before they gasp.

---

## SERIES: SEEN IN SILVERBRIDGE

**Core Premise:** A group of young investigative journalists run a 'gossipy' media outlet (_Seen in Silverbridge_) and repeatedly find themselves uncovering high-level conspiracies within elite institutions.

Each book features:

- A high-concept inciting death or scandal — rendered as a vivid, almost cinematic event
- An elite environment (celebrity, sport, media, wealth, influence)
- Moral conflict between loyalty and truth
- Escalating danger
- Emotional tension within the friend group

**Series arc:** Six books. Institutional corruption escalates across the series. Personal stakes rise with it.

**Novella:** _A Student Has Drowned_ (prequel / series entry point)

**Book titles follow a declarative headline format** — e.g. _A Rockstar Has Exploded_, _An Actress Is Missing_, _A Fiancé Has Flatlined. Titles read like breaking news alerts written by the protagonists themselves.

## THEMATIC CORE
Emerson Black stories are built on this philosophy:

> Friendship and being "seen" by trusted peers can get you through any hardship.

Additional thematic pillars:

- Loyalty under pressure
- The corruption of elite institutions
- Truth vs personal comfort
- Romance complicates justice
- Identity shaped by community
- Young people navigating adult power structures
- Social media's pervasive influence on truth and reputation
- Empathy as a radical act — seeing the full person behind the public image

Mysteries are emotional before procedural. The crime matters — but the relationships matter more. The reader should feel the tension between wanting to protect the people they love and wanting to do what is right.

## TONE

The tone of Seen in Silverbridge is best understood as a swing between two registers that the books hold simultaneously:

**Register 1 — Warm and witty.** The characters are funny. The dialogue crackles. There is genuine lightness, especially in the early sections of each book. Readers should feel the pull of this friend group — the desire to be in it — before the danger arrives.

**Register 2 — Genuinely dangerous.** When the stakes land, they land hard. The shift from warm to dangerous within a single scene is one of the series' core pleasures and should never be softened in marketing copy or AI-generated content.

The combination of these two registers is the series' primary selling proposition. It is what makes it feel like:

> _A Good Girl's Guide to Murder_ meets _Only Murders in the Building_ — with the friendship dynamics of a found-family ensemble and the glossy world of elite institutions.

**Additional tone descriptors:**

- Fast-paced
- Cinematic
- Character-driven (not "soapy" — the drama earns its emotion)
- Slightly racy without being explicit
- Mysterious and intriguing
- Contemporary and grounded
- Witty — the protagonist's voice has comic timing

## PROTAGONIST DYNAMIC (SERIES DNA)

The core four characters are the emotional spine of the series. Their dynamic — four very different people who become an unlikely team and genuinely come to care for each other — is the primary reason readers return.

- **Luce Sharp** — 22. Gorgeous, sharp-tongued, and socially fearless. Driven by ambition and complicated by conscience. Torn between love and truth. The engine of the group.
- **Hudson "Huds" Haile** — 22. Intelligent, kind, and quietly reckless. The moral counterweight to Luce. His emotional conflict — between what is right and what will protect the people he loves — is central to the series.
- **Faven Osmun** — 19. Shy, insecure, and strategically brilliant. An immigrant student finding her footing. Her perspective cuts through the social noise the others are too close to see.
- **Rodney Holliday** — 48. Cynical, fearless, and down-at-heel. A veteran investigative journalist who has seen it all and lost most of it. The moral anchor and the reluctant adult in the room.

Internal conflict between these four is as important as the mystery itself. The reader should feel genuinely uncertain, at times, whether the group will hold together.

- Glossy campus / elite environment thriller

**The readership you are reaching for:** Fans of Holly Jackson, Jennifer Lynn Barnes, Karen M. McManus, and the _Only Murders in the Building_ audience. Female-skewed, ages 16–25. Readers who want to care about characters as much as they want to solve the mystery.

## Cover Design
**Core Concept:** A singular, iconic figure or "victim of the week" rendered in high-contrast vector art, paired with a cinematic symbol of the inciting event. The style is "Noir-Pop"—sophisticated, minimalist, and urgent.
**Typography:**
- **Title:** Bold, all-caps sans-serif. It must dominate the top third and read like a breaking news headline (e.g., _A ROCKSTAR HAS EXPLODED_) in aggressive red.
- **Series Line:** Wide-tracked small caps positioned at the very top (e.g., SEEN IN SILVERBRIDGE EXCLUSIVE // BOOK 1).
- **Author Name:** Century Gothic Bold at the bottom.
**Cover Tone:**
- **Slightly Dangerous:** Uses heavy shadows and sharp angles to imply a world of "scandal and power structures".
- **Minimalist:** Relies on negative space and a limited color palette rather than cluttered detail.
**Color Palette Strategy:  Monochromatic.

**Avoid:**
- **Cartoonish/Illustrated YA:** No "bubbly" lines or cute character art.
- **Gradients:** Stick to flat color blocks and sharp transitions.
- **Clutter:** If an element doesn't add to the "Breaking News" feel, cut it.

## WHAT TO AVOID WHEN GENERATING CONTENT
- Overly graphic violence
- Smutty or explicit romance framing
- Middle-grade or childish tone
- Hard-boiled detective noir tone
- Excessive cynicism or ironic detachment
- Undermining friendship as the series' core emotional strength
- Using _Gossip Girl_ as the primary tonal reference
- Writing Luce as purely mean rather than sharp and funny
- Writing Huds as passive — he is kind but also reckless and morally complex
